# Hedonic Pricing Model: Environmental Shocks and Property Valuation

To assess how historical environmental shocks impacted property values and post-disaster development, we use a Hedonic Pricing Model. A hedonic model deconstructs a property's total price into its individual components. It assumes that buyers do not just buy a 'house'; they buy a bundle of attributes (e.g., square footage, school district, and environmental safety).

---

## The Hedonic Pricing Equation

In real estate economics, the hedonic price (P) is a function of Structural characteristics (S), Neighborhood characteristics (N), and Environmental characteristics (E).

## Pipeline Strategy: 4 Historical Case Studies

---

### 1. The Great Smog of London (1952)

- **The Disaster:**  
A severe air pollution event that trapped toxic smog over London, causing thousands of deaths.

- **Hedonic Assessment & Development:**  
Before the 1950s, prevailing westerly winds pushed factory smoke to the East End. The hedonic model of London historically priced East End properties much lower (the 'pollution penalty'). Following the disaster, the Clean Air Act of 1956 was passed.

- **Post-Event Reality:**  
As the environmental penalty (δₖ) of bad air quality decreased due to regulation, the East End experienced massive, long-term redevelopment and gentrification. The hedonic value of air quality equalized across the city.

---

### 2. Louisiana Hurricane Katrina (2005)

- **The Disaster:**  
Catastrophic flooding and levee failure that inundated New Orleans.

- **Hedonic Assessment & Development:**  
Katrina completely broke the existing housing market. In the immediate aftermath, the hedonic penalty for being located below sea level became the single most dominant factor in property valuation.

- **Post-Event Reality:**  
Development shifted sharply. A massive hedonic premium emerged for Base Flood Elevation (BFE). Homes that were raised above the floodplain saw their values recover and surge, while ground-level slab homes in hard-hit wards suffered permanent value destruction.

---

### 3. Palisades Fire, Los Angeles (2021)

- **The Disaster:**  
A highly visible brush fire in one of the wealthiest enclaves of Southern California.

- **Hedonic Assessment & Development:**  
The Pacific Palisades is unique because its Neighborhood (N) and Structural (S) coefficients are incredibly high (ocean views, luxury estates). Historically, the view premium far outweighed the fire risk penalty.

- **Post-Event Reality:**  
The market response is currently playing out through insurance rather than immediate price crashes. The hedonic penalty is manifesting as the cost of mandatory brush clearance, fire-resistant materials, and soaring carrying costs. Development hasn't stopped, but new builds are now essentially concrete-and-steel bunkers disguised as luxury homes.

---

### 4. Hurricane Sandy, New York/New Jersey (2012)

- **The Disaster:**  
A massive storm surge that devastated the Northeastern US coastline.

- **Hedonic Assessment & Development:**  
Before Sandy, proximity to the water was a pure hedonic premium. After Sandy, FEMA redrew the flood maps.

- **Post-Event Reality:**  
If a property was placed into a new 'V-Zone' (high velocity wave action), it triggered mandatory, exorbitant flood insurance unless the house was lifted. The hedonic model split: homes that invested in resilience (raising the foundation) retained their waterfront premium, while unmitigated homes took an average 15–20% hit to their market value.
